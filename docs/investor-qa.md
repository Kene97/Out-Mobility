# Investor Q&A — Out Mobility

**Format:** Question as asked (not softened). Answer as given (direct, not defensive).
**Principle:** The best investors ask the hardest questions. Welcome them.
**Rule:** Never answer a question you weren't asked. Never dodge a question you were.

---

## Category 1: "Is this real?"

### Q: Why won't Uber just do this?

**Answer:**
"Two reasons. First, Uber Ads is an in-app product — they sell advertising
inside the Uber app to passengers browsing on their phone. That's a different
surface, a different product, and a different team. They would be building a
physical hardware operations company from scratch, which is antithetical to
their asset-light model.

Second, Uber's relationships in Nigeria are with drivers, not with fleet operators.
Their driver agreements explicitly limit what drivers can put in their vehicles.
Mandating hardware at scale would create driver relations issues at exactly
the moment they're fighting Bolt for market share.

More fundamentally: the in-vehicle physical screen business requires field ops,
device management, and fleet relationships that Uber doesn't have and doesn't want.
They are a software company. We are an infrastructure company."

---

### Q: Can't a smart team in Lagos just copy this in 6 months?

**Answer:**
"Yes, they can write the software in 6 months. Maybe faster.

What they can't do in 6 months: build operator trust, deploy 1,000 devices,
generate a data moat of 100M+ verified impressions, or establish the advertiser
relationships that make fill rate work.

The software is 20% of the business. The other 80% is fleet operations, operator
relationships, advertiser trust, and the verification dataset — all of which
compound with time and cannot be bought.

By the time a copycat reaches 500 devices, we'll be at 5,000. The window to catch
up closes with every week we operate."

---

### Q: Isn't this just a hardware company with thin margins?

**Answer:**
"No. And this is an important distinction.

We don't manufacture hardware. We don't own hardware. Operators own the tablets.
We build the software platform: the advertiser dashboard, the campaign scheduler,
the verification engine, the device management system.

Our cost per device per month is $1 — CDN bandwidth, database, support.
Our revenue per device at Scale is $12.55 per month. That's 92% gross margin.

The hardware is the operator's capital expenditure, recovered through revenue share.
We are a software infrastructure company with 92% gross margins that happens to
deploy its product into physical vehicles."

---

### Q: How do you know advertisers will actually pay for this?

**Answer:**
"We're not assuming they will. We're requiring that one pays before we close
this round.

The pre-seed funds the 90-day plan. Day 30: first devices live.
Day 45: first campaign running. Day 60: verified impression report delivered.
If an advertiser writes a check after seeing a verified report — and we believe
they will, because Nigerian brands are desperate for accountable media —
then we have proof. If they don't write the check, we have a serious learning
that will reshape the approach.

We are not raising on the assumption that advertisers will pay. We are raising
to find out."

## Category 2: "Does the model work?"

### Q: What is your CAC for advertisers and what is the payback period?

**Answer:**
"Pilot CAC: approximately $400 per advertiser. This covers founder time,
outreach, and the manual onboarding cost for the first 5 advertisers.

Average pilot campaign: $2,000. Out Mobility 65% take: $1,300.
Campaign duration: 3 weeks. Monthly margin from one advertiser: ~$1,733.

CAC payback: $400 / $1,733 = under 3 months. Usually under the first campaign.

At Scale stage, CAC rises to ~$800 as we build a sales team.
But campaign sizes also rise to $4,000+. Payback stays under 3 months.

The advertiser-side economics are not the constraint. The constraint is
acquisition velocity — how fast we can close deals, not whether we make
money on them once closed."

---

### Q: What is the payback period per device?

**Answer:**
"At Scale economics — $7.50 CPM, 60% fill rate — we contribute $11.55
per device per month (net of our $1/month platform cost).

The device costs the operator $120 to install. The operator earns $6.76/month
at Scale. Their hardware payback is 18 months. That's fine for them —
any income from a screen they didn't previously have is additive.

For Out Mobility: our cost to onboard a new device is ~$30 in admin time.
Our monthly contribution is $11.55. Our payback: 2.6 months.

This is why device count is the primary growth lever. At 1,000 devices,
we contribute $11,550/month against $14,900/month in fixed costs.
At 1,300 devices, we break even. At 2,000 devices, we're generating
strong free cash flow."

---

### Q: What if CPM comes in at $4 instead of $6?

**Answer:**
"At $4 CPM with 30% fill, revenue per device drops to $5.14/month.
Out Mobility's 65% take: $3.34. After $1 platform cost: $2.34 contribution.
Breakeven would require ~6,400 devices instead of ~800.

That's a serious problem. But here's the context:

One: $4 CPM is below current DOOH CPMs in Lagos ($4–8).
We should not be below market — we're offering a better product.

Two: If we can't achieve $5+ CPM, it means advertisers don't value
verification — and that invalidates the entire thesis. At that point, we've
learned something important and we'd need to revisit the model.

Three: Our opening offer is $6. If the market pushes back below $5,
we hear that signal early (in the 90-day pilot) before deploying 1,000 devices.
The pilot is specifically designed to validate CPM assumptions."

---

### Q: How do you handle device theft or damage?

**Answer:**
"Operator accountability. The operator signs an MOU making them responsible
for the physical device. The device is their asset from a revenue perspective
— damaging or losing it hurts their income, not just ours.

Practically: devices are mounted in the vehicle and powered by vehicle power.
They're not easily removed. The kiosk lock means they have no resale value
as a general-purpose tablet. Theft is low-incentive.

For genuine losses (accidents, vehicle theft): we don't replace free of charge.
We charge at cost ($80–100). At scale, we'll offer device insurance as an
add-on for operators who want it.

Our expected device churn is <2%/month. At 1,000 devices, that's 20 devices/month
to replace. A manageable operational cost."

## Category 3: "What kills this?"

### Q: What is the biggest risk to this business?

**Answer (honest):**
"Advertiser demand. Everything else we can solve.

If Nigerian advertisers consistently refuse to pay more than $3 CPM for
in-vehicle inventory — even verified inventory — then the unit economics
don't work at scale.

We don't believe this is the likely outcome. Nigerian brands like MTN, Dangote,
and Access Bank are sophisticated buyers who understand verified media.
The agencies that represent them — Insight BBDO, Noah's Ark — buy DOOH
at $4–8 CPM with far less accountability. We offer more accountability.

But this is the thesis to validate in the 90-day pilot. We are not raising
$3M on the assumption that the thesis is right. We are raising $200K
to test whether it is."

---

### Q: What if devices keep going offline in the field?

**Answer:**
"Two-part answer.

First: the device is designed to work offline. It caches ad content locally,
continues playback, and syncs logs when network is restored. An offline
device still delivers impressions — it just reports them with a delay.
We've designed around Nigeria's network reality from the start.

Second: if a device is offline for >2 hours, the admin gets an alert.
We (or the operator) can physically check on it. At 50 devices, this is
manageable. At 5,000, we need field operations support — which is in the
Series A hiring plan.

Uptime target: >85%. At 85%, the unit economics still work.
At 70%, they get tight. At 60%, we have an operational problem to solve."

---

### Q: What happens if Bolt or a local company copies this tomorrow?

**Answer:**
"Good. Validation of the category helps us.

But realistically: by the time a competitor deploys 200 devices, we have 2,000.
By the time they have 5 advertisers, we have 50. The data moat widens every day.

The most dangerous competitor is one that starts today with serious capital.
That's why we're asking for money now — to build the fleet density that
makes us uncatchable before a well-funded competitor enters."

## Category 4: "Why you / why now?"

### Q: Why Nigeria first? Why not the US or UK?

**Answer:**
"Three reasons.

One: Nigeria has no incumbent. In the US, Alfi and Vugo have been operating
for years with limited traction. The category is established but not dominated.
In Nigeria, the category doesn't exist. We are the category.

Two: Nigeria has structural advantages for this model — high traffic density,
rapidly formalizing ride-hail fleet, and advertisers who currently have no
good verification options. The problem is acute. The solution is urgent.

Three: founder-market fit. Building at scale in Lagos requires relationships,
operational knowledge, and credibility that a foreign founder can't buy quickly.
We have that. A well-funded US startup does not."

**Answer:**

$9,000–15,000 MRR. At least one campaign renewal from 70%+ of advertisers.
Device uptime >85% fleet-wide.

At those metrics, we have a fundable seed round at $10M pre-money.

— which is the Series A.

The path is clear. The question is execution speed."

## Category 5: "The numbers"

### Q: Your market size seems small. $50M SAM?

**Answer:**
"It's a conscious choice to be conservative.

$50M is what we believe we can capture, not what the global market 'is.'
The global in-vehicle advertising market is $8–12B. Nigeria is a beachhead,
not the destination.

If we build the platform right in Lagos — the infrastructure, the data moat,
the operator network, the advertiser trust — the same playbook runs in Nairobi,
Cairo, Accra, and eventually Southeast Asia. Every city with a formal ride-hail
fleet and advertisers who want verification is an addressable market.

The company we're building is worth far more than the $50M SAM if we execute.
But we're not pitching the global market — we're pitching Lagos. That's deliberate.
Investors who've been burned by inflated TAMs deserve honesty."

---

### Q: Show me the sensitivity. What breaks the model?

**Answer:**
"Three things that break the model:

One: CPM below $4. If advertisers won't pay $4, the unit economics collapse.
We learn this in the 90-day pilot. Low risk of being surprised — we've already
validated appetite with target brands informally.

Two: Fill rate below 20% at 500 devices. That means advertiser demand is structurally
weak. Our fix: more sales effort, different advertiser segments, lower minimum spends.

Three: Device uptime below 70%. At that point, operators are losing revenue
and advertiser confidence drops. Fix: better MDM, tighter SLAs, field ops.

What doesn't break the model: slower device acquisition (just delays breakeven),
higher CAC (just reduces margin on demand side), or higher platform costs
(our $1/device/month is a conservative estimate — it may be even lower).

The pilot is designed to stress-test the three kill conditions before we scale."

## How to Use This Q&A

**Before investor meetings:** Read through once. Don't memorize — internalize.

**During meetings:** Never volunteer a question and answer. Wait for the question.

**When asked something not here:** Say "That's a great question. Let me think about
it for a second" — and think for a second. A considered answer is better than
a rehearsed one.

**On numbers:** Only cite numbers you can defend to two decimal places. Round numbers
signal estimation. Specific numbers signal rigor.

**On confidence:** Certainty about the product. Honesty about the risks.
These are not contradictory. The best founders know what they know
and know what they don't.

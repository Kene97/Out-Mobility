# Investor Narrative — Out Mobility

**The case for why this wins. Written for a Series A VC who has never heard of us.**

---

## The Core Thesis

There are 350 million people in West Africa who spend an average of 90 minutes per day
in vehicles. None of that attention is systematically monetized.

Out Mobility is building the infrastructure layer that changes that — starting with
the screen inside the vehicle, and expanding into every dimension of the mobility stack.

**Out-door is the first product. It is not the whole company.**

---

## Why Now

Four things are simultaneously true for the first time:

**1. The fleet exists.** Ride-hailing formalized the vehicle fleet.
Lagos alone has 200,000+ formal ride-hail vehicles with GPS, smartphones, and data.
This fleet did not exist in 2015.

**2. The hardware is cheap enough.** A 4G Android tablet with GPS, capable of verified
video delivery, now costs $80–100 wholesale. The cost of the "screen in every car"
problem dropped 10x in 5 years.

**3. The advertisers are desperate.** Digital CPMs are rising. Feed saturation is real.
Nigerian brands — MTN, Dangote, Access Bank, Guinness — are actively looking for
verified, high-attention alternatives. They have the budget. They lack the product.

**4. No one has built the infrastructure.** Uber hasn't. The telcos haven't.
The billboard companies haven't. The category is open.

The window is 2025–2028. After that, it will be crowded.

## Why This Is a Network Effect Business

**Supply-side network effect (fleet → fleet):**
More operators join when they see peer operators earning.
In ride-hail networks, word-of-mouth among drivers is the fastest growth channel.
One operator with 100 vehicles earning ₦50,000/month is a better sales pitch than any deck.

**Demand-side network effect (advertisers → advertisers):**
More advertisers join when campaigns perform well for early clients.
A bank sees a telecom's CPM and conversion data, and they want in.
Repeat advertisers bring peers. Brand managers talk.

**Cross-side network effect (fleet ↔ advertisers):**
More vehicles → more impressions available → more advertisers join.
More advertisers → better fill rate → operators earn more → more operators join.
This is a classic liquidity loop. Once it starts, it compounds.

**Data network effect:**
Every impression logged strengthens the verification data.
Every trip added to the GPS database improves targeting precision.
The dataset becomes more valuable with every device deployed.
A competitor starting from scratch in Year 3 is 3 years behind on data quality.

## The Moat Analysis

### 1. Data Moat
**What it is:** Trip-level GPS + device telemetry for every impression.
**Why it compounds:** The verification dataset grows with every impression.
More data → better fraud detection → higher verified impression quality → higher CPM premium.
**Replication difficulty:** Requires 2+ years of live fleet data to match. Cannot be bought.

### 2. Operator Lock-In (Distribution Moat)
**What it is:** Fleet operators who have installed Out-door tablets have switching costs.
**Why it matters:** Removing tablets costs money, time, and lost revenue.
A competing platform would need to offer substantially better economics to get operators to switch.
**Replication difficulty:** Takes 12–18 months to sign enough operators to offer comparable reach.

### 3. Advertiser Trust Moat
**What it is:** The first advertiser to see a verified impression report becomes a reference.
**Why it matters:** Enterprise brands need verified data. Once a CMO has seen ours,
unverified alternatives feel irresponsible. We become the default.
**Replication difficulty:** Trust is not copyable. It's earned through delivered campaigns.

### 4. Network Density Moat
**What it is:** At 5,000 devices in Lagos, we cover most major routes.
**Why it matters:** Advertisers need reach. A competitor with 200 devices cannot compete.
Coverage is a binary threshold: below it, you can't sell scale campaigns.
**Replication difficulty:** Capital intensive. Requires $600K+ in device subsidy + operator relationships.

### 5. Operational Moat
**What it is:** The logistics of deploying, managing, and maintaining a device fleet
at scale in emerging markets is genuinely hard.
**Why it matters:** No software company can replicate this without 12–18 months of field operations.
**Replication difficulty:** You can write the software in 6 months.
You cannot build the operator relationships, the field support system,
and the device management playbook in under a year.

## Why Competitors Cannot Easily Replicate

### Why Uber / Bolt Won't Build This
- Uber Ads is an in-app product — different surface, different business model
- Building physical hardware ops is antithetical to their asset-light model
- Their fleet is their supply — they won't complicate driver relationships with hardware mandates
- In Nigeria specifically: Uber and Bolt are still fighting for fare market share

### Why Billboard Companies Won't Build This
- OOH companies are not software companies
- They have no device management capability, no data infrastructure
- Physical asset model (they own boards) is the opposite of our capital-light model

### Why Telcos Won't Build This
- MTN, Airtel have the data, but not the ad-tech or the fleet relationships
- Their core business is connectivity — this is too far from core

### Why a New Startup Shouldn't Just Copy This
- 18-month head start on data
- Operator relationships locked in
- Advertiser trust is the first-mover asset
- By the time a competitor reaches 500 devices, we're at 5,000
